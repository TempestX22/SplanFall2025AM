ABOUT

The shiitake (Japanese: 椎茸), scientific name Lentinula edodes, is a macrofungus native to East Asia,
widely cultivated and consumed around the world. It grows on decaying hardwood logs (especially oak, chestnut, etc.)
and is prized for its rich flavor and aroma. The species was first described as Agaricus edodes by Miles
Joseph Berkeley in 1877, and later reassigned to the genus Lentinula by David Pegler in 1976.

TAXONOMY

Genus: Lentinula
Species: edodes
Authority: (Berk.) Pegler (1976)
Synonyms / earlier names: Agaricus edodes, Armillaria edodes, Lentinus edodes, and more.

MORPHOLOGY & ECOLOGY

The cap size ranges roughly from 5 to 25 cm across; when young it is darker
and then tends toward brown as it matures. The gills are free, the stipe
is bare and fibrous, and the spore print is white. Ecologically, shiitake
is saprotrophic, decomposing dead hardwoods (e.g. oak), breaking down cellulose and lignin.

USES & SIGNIFICANCE

Shiitake is one of the most commonly cultivated edible mushrooms globally.
It is valued in cuisine for its umami flavor, and in traditional medicine
and research for bioactive compounds such as lentinan (a β-glucan) which
is studied for potential immunomodulatory effects.
In some countries, lentinan is used as an adjunctive therapy in cancer treatment.

COMMON NAMES

Shiitake, fragrant mushroom, Chinese black mushroom
